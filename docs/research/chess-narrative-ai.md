# Chess Narrative AI: Extracting Strategic Understanding from Game Data

**Status:** Research proposal
**Author:** Darrell Thomas
**Date:** 2026-03-22
**Project:** En Parlant~

---

## Vision

Every chess game ever played contains a hidden narrative — the strategic reasoning behind each move. This reasoning is not written down for the vast majority of games, but it is **implicitly encoded** in the move sequences themselves. Millions of games playing the same opening, navigating the same middlegame structures, and converting the same endgames form natural clusters in a latent space. These clusters *are* the strategic concepts.

En Parlant~ sits on 9M+ games with full move sequences, and now (as of PR #710) preserves annotations, comments, and NAGs through the database pipeline. The goal is to build an AI system that:

1. **Discovers** strategic concepts from the raw game data via unsupervised learning
2. **Narrates** any game by mapping its moves to discovered concepts
3. **Speaks** the narration through TTS in configurable styles

No chess application has ever done this. Engine analysis tells you *what* is good or bad. We want to explain *why* — and tell the story.

---

## Core Insight: The Hidden Markov Structure

Chess games are sequences of observable moves generated by unobservable strategic reasoning:

```
Hidden states:   [develop] → [control center] → [prepare kingside attack] → [sacrifice!] → [convert]
                      ↓              ↓                    ↓                      ↓              ↓
Observable moves:   Nf3            d4                    f4                    Bxh7+           Qh5
```

The hidden states are the *thoughts* behind the moves. Different players traverse different hidden state sequences even in identical positions — that's what "style" is.

**Key claim:** These hidden states do not need to be manually defined or labeled. They emerge naturally from clustering millions of game trajectories. The annotations that exist in some games are confirming evidence, not training labels.

---

## Why Unsupervised

The strategic structure is already embedded in the data:

- **A million King's Indian games** all share a DNA: Black fianchettoes, allows White a space advantage, then counter-attacks on the kingside. No one needs to label this — it's a dense cluster in move-sequence space.

- **Within that cluster**, sub-patterns emerge: the Bayonet Attack, the Classical Variation, the Saemisch — each with its own sub-cluster of characteristic move patterns and positional themes.

- **Cross-opening patterns** also cluster: minority attacks appear in the QGD, the Carlsbad structure, and certain Ruy Lopez lines. These are the same strategic concept manifesting in different opening contexts.

Supervised labels from annotations are useful for *naming* discovered clusters and for validation, but the clusters exist independently. The model should discover structure first, then annotations help interpret and label what was found.

## Architecture

### Phase 1: Game Embedding

**Input:** Move sequence (from database, 1 byte per move via legal-move indexing)

**Process:** Convert each game into a fixed-dimensional vector that captures its strategic essence.

**Approach: Position-sequence autoencoder**

1. Decode moves into position snapshots at regular intervals (every 5 plies)
2. Each position encoded as a feature vector:
   - Piece placement (12 × 64 = 768 binary features, or compressed via piece-square learned embeddings)
   - Material balance (scalar)
   - Pawn structure (bitboard hash or learned embedding)
   - King positions (safety proxy)
   - Castling rights
   - Move number (game phase proxy)
3. Sequence of position vectors fed through a small transformer encoder or LSTM
4. Pooled into a single game embedding (e.g., 512 or 1024 dimensions)
5. Trained via autoencoder objective: reconstruct the position sequence from the embedding

The autoencoder forces the embedding to capture the essential trajectory of the game — what changed and when.

**Alternative: Contrastive learning**

Instead of reconstruction, use contrastive learning:
- Positive pairs: different games in the same opening (ECO code) or same player
- Negative pairs: random games
- The model learns to place similar games nearby without explicit labels

### Phase 2: Clustering & Concept Discovery

**Input:** Game embeddings for all 9M+ games

**Process:** Discover the natural strategic vocabulary.

1. **Hierarchical clustering** on the embedding space:
   - Level 0: Broad families (open games, closed games, Indian systems, flank openings)
   - Level 1: Opening complexes (Ruy Lopez family, Sicilian family, Queen's Gambit family)
   - Level 2: Structural plans (minority attack, kingside pawn storm, central breakthrough)
   - Level 3: Tactical motifs (exchange sacrifice on c3, bishop sacrifice on h7, rook lift)

2. **Temporal clustering** — not just whole-game embeddings, but embeddings at different game phases:
   - Opening (moves 1-15): formation choices
   - Middlegame (moves 15-35): plan execution
   - Endgame (moves 35+): conversion technique

   A game might belong to cluster A in the opening but cluster B in the middlegame (e.g., a quiet Ruy Lopez that turns into a sharp tactical battle).

3. **Transition patterns** — which opening clusters tend to lead to which middlegame clusters? This IS the strategic model. "When you play the Breyer Ruy Lopez, you typically end up in a maneuvering middlegame (cluster M7) but occasionally it sharpens into a kingside attack (cluster M12) when White plays f4."

### Phase 3: Concept Labeling

**Input:** Discovered clusters + existing annotated games

**Process:** Give human-readable names to discovered concepts.

1. For each cluster, find the annotated games within it
2. Extract common themes from their comments (NLP):
   - Cluster contains 50 annotated games, 35 mention "weak d-pawn" → label: "Targeting isolated d-pawn"
   - Cluster contains 80 annotated games, 60 mention "king safety" or "attack" → label: "Kingside attacking formation"
3. For clusters with no annotations, use positional features:
   - All games in cluster have pawns on d5+e4 → "Classical center"
   - All games in cluster have opposite-side castling → "Opposite-side attack"

This is where annotations (PR #710) become invaluable — not as training labels, but as a Rosetta Stone for interpreting what the model discovered.

### Phase 4: Narration Generation

**Input:** A game + its embedding trajectory + cluster assignments at each phase

**Process:** Generate natural language narration.

1. At each move (or key moment), the model identifies:
   - Which strategic cluster the game is in
   - Whether a transition is happening (plan change)
   - Whether the move is typical for this cluster (thematic) or unusual (surprise/novelty)
   - The eval trajectory (improving? worsening? critical moment?)

2. Template-based narration (v1):
   ```
   "White develops the knight to f3, preparing to control the center.
   This is a typical Ruy Lopez formation — we'll see the battle for
   d5 become the central theme. [Move matches cluster: Ruy Lopez
   Classical, confidence 0.94]"
   ```

3. LLM-enhanced narration (v2):
   Feed the strategic state sequence + position features to a fine-tuned language model that generates fluid commentary. The cluster assignments provide the *what to say*; the language model provides the *how to say it*.

### Phase 5: Style Transfer

**Input:** Narration + target style (Kasparov, Karpov, Tal, etc.)

**Process:** Modulate the narration to reflect different analytical perspectives.

Each master's annotated games define a **style signature** — not just vocabulary, but what they choose to comment on:
- Kasparov annotates dynamics, initiative, time (tempo)
- Karpov annotates structure, prophylaxis, long-term weaknesses
- Tal annotates sacrifices, complications, psychological pressure
- Fischer annotates precision, refutations, preparation

The style transfer model learns: given the same strategic state, what would each commentator emphasize?

## Technical Requirements

### Compute

- **Embedding generation:** GPU recommended for transformer-based approach. CPU feasible for feature-engineered approach. One-time cost per database.
- **Clustering:** CPU-bound, parallelizable. 9M × 1024-dim vectors fit in ~36GB RAM.
- **Inference (narration):** Must run on user's machine. Small ONNX model (~100-200MB) for embedding + lookup. Narration generation could be local (small LLM) or API-based.

### Storage

- **Vector index:** ~36GB for 9M games at 1024-dim float32 (mmap-friendly)
- **Cluster assignments:** ~100MB (cluster IDs per game per phase)
- **Concept dictionary:** ~1MB (cluster labels and descriptions)
- **Style models:** ~50-200MB per style (fine-tuned narration parameters)

### Integration with En Parlant~

- ONNX Runtime already bundled (KittenTTS)
- Database pipeline already preserves annotations (PR #710)
- TTS infrastructure already in place (ElevenLabs, Google Cloud, KittenTTS)
- Move decoding to text already implemented (`decode_game_to_movetext`)

## Open Questions

1. **Embedding granularity:** Whole-game embeddings vs. per-move embeddings vs. per-phase embeddings? Per-phase seems most useful for narration (you need to know what's happening *now*, not just the game's overall character).

2. **Position representation:** Raw piece placement (768-dim binary) vs. learned piece-square embeddings vs. Stockfish NNUE hidden layers (which already encode positional understanding)?

3. **Temporal modeling:** Transformer (attention over full game) vs. LSTM (sequential) vs. Mamba (selective state space)? Games have strong sequential structure but also long-range dependencies (a pawn move on move 10 determines the endgame at move 40).

4. **Eval integration:** Should engine evaluation be an input feature, a training signal, or kept separate? Risk: the model might learn to parrot eval numbers rather than discover strategic concepts.

5. **Novelty detection:** How to identify when a game diverges from its cluster? This is where narration gets interesting — "Here Black deviates from the standard plan..."

6. **Real-time vs. batch:** Can embedding + narration run fast enough for live game commentary? Or is this a post-game analysis tool?

7. **What counts as "similar"?** Same opening? Same pawn structure? Same eval trajectory? Same strategic plan? The answer determines the embedding architecture.

8. **Cross-era analysis:** Chess strategy has evolved. A "good" plan in 1960 might be refuted by 2025 standards. Should the model account for temporal context?

## References & Prior Art

- **Leela Chess Zero:** Self-play learned chess representation. The policy and value networks contain rich positional understanding.
- **NNUE (Efficiently Updatable Neural Networks):** Stockfish's evaluation function. Piece-square tables learned from self-play. Could be repurposed as position embeddings.
- **Chess Transformers:** Various research papers on transformer models for chess (move prediction, position evaluation). Typically trained on Lichess data.
- **Hidden Markov Models in games:** Established framework for modeling sequential decision-making with latent state.
- **Word2Vec/GloVe analogy:** Just as words get meaning from context, moves get meaning from surrounding moves and positions. "Nf3" in a Ruy Lopez means something different than "Nf3" in a London System.
